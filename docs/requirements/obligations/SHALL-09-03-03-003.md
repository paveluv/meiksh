## Extracted Text

> A BRE special character has special properties in certain contexts. Outside those contexts, or when preceded by an unescaped <backslash>, such a character is a BRE that matches the special character itself. The BRE special characters and the contexts in which they have their special meaning are as follows:: The <circumflex> shall be special when used as an anchor (see 9.3.8 BRE Expression Anchoring ). The <circumflex> shall signify a non-matching list expression when it occurs first in a list, immediately following a <left-square-bracket> (see 9.3.5 RE Bracket Expression).

## Context

**Behavior:** In BRE context, the circumflex (`^`) shall be special in two contexts: (1) as an anchor when used at the beginning of the BRE (see 9.3.8), and (2) to signify a non-matching list when it occurs first in a bracket expression list, immediately following `[` (see 9.3.5).

**Shell relevance:** In shell pattern matching, `^` is not used as an anchor (patterns match the whole string in `case`, or are applied per filename component in globbing). However, `^` IS significant inside bracket expressions: `[^abc]` (or `[!abc]` in shell notation) defines a non-matching list. Section 9.3.5 specifies that both `^` and `!` can be used as the negation character in bracket expressions within shell patterns.
