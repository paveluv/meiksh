## Extracted Text

> For the purposes of this section, the term "IFS white space" is used to mean any of the white-space bytes (see XBD 3.413 White Space, 3.414 White-Space Byte, and 3.415 White-Space Character) <space>, <tab>, or <newline> from the portable character set (see XBD 6.1 Portable Character Set) which are present in the value of the IFS variable, and perhaps other white-space characters. It is implementation-defined whether other white-space characters which appear in the value of IFS are also considered as "IFS white space". The three characters above specified as IFS white-space bytes are always IFS white space, when they occur in the value of IFS , regardless of whether they are white-space characters in any relevant locale. For other locale-specific white-space characters allowed by the implementation it is unspecified whether the character is considered as IFS white space if it is white space at the time it is assigned to the IFS variable, or if it is white space at the time field splitting occurs. (The locale might have changed between those events.)

## Context

**Behavior:** This defines "IFS white space" — a critical concept for the field splitting algorithm, where IFS white space characters behave differently from non-white-space IFS delimiters:

**Guaranteed IFS white space (always):**
- Space (0x20)
- Tab (0x09)
- Newline (0x0A)

These three are ALWAYS considered IFS white space when present in IFS, regardless of locale.

**Implementation-defined:** Whether other white-space characters in the current locale (e.g., vertical tab, form feed, non-breaking space) are also treated as "IFS white space" when they appear in IFS. meiksh must document its choice.

**Unspecified:** For locale-specific white-space characters (beyond the three guaranteed ones), if the locale changes between the time IFS is assigned and field splitting occurs, it is unspecified whether the character's white-space status is determined at assignment time or splitting time.

**Why it matters:** IFS white space characters are treated as field *terminators* that coalesce (multiple adjacent ones act as one delimiter). Non-white-space IFS characters are treated as explicit delimiters where each occurrence produces a field boundary (see SHALL-19-06-05-012).

**Spec reference:** See 2.6.5 Field Splitting in utilities/V3_chap02.html, lines 1222–1233. See basedefs/V1_chap03.html for definitions: 3.413 White Space, 3.414 White-Space Byte, 3.415 White-Space Character. See basedefs/V1_chap06.html#tag_06_01 for Portable Character Set.
