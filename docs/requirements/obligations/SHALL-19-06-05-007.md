## Extracted Text

> The shell shall use the byte sequences that form the characters in the value of the IFS variable as delimiters. Each of the characters <space>, <tab>, and <newline> which appears in the value of IFS shall be a single-byte delimiter. The shell shall use these delimiters as field terminators to split the results of expansions, along with other adjacent bytes, into separate fields, as described below. Note that these delimiters terminate a field; they do not, of themselves, cause a new field to start—subsequent bytes that are not from the results of an expansion, or that do not form IFS white-space characters are required for a new field to begin.

## Context

**Behavior:** IFS character handling rules:

1. **Characters as delimiters:** Each character in IFS acts as a field delimiter. For multi-byte characters, the entire byte sequence forming that character is the delimiter.
2. **Space/tab/newline are always single-byte:** Even in multi-byte locales, space (0x20), tab (0x09), and newline (0x0A) are always single-byte delimiters.
3. **Terminators, not separators:** IFS delimiters **terminate** fields — they do not start new fields by themselves. This is a critical distinction:
   - After consuming an IFS delimiter, a new field only begins when a non-IFS-white-space byte is encountered (either a literal byte not from an expansion, or an expansion byte that is not IFS white space).
   - This means leading IFS white space does not produce an empty leading field.
   - Trailing IFS white space does not produce an empty trailing field.

**Spec reference:** See 2.6.5 Field Splitting in utilities/V3_chap02.html, lines 1237–1242.
