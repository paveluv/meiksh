## Extracted Text

> Execution of a suspended job can be continued as a foreground job by means of the fg utility (if supported), or as a (non-suspended) background job either by means of the bg utility (if supported) or by sending the stopped processes a SIGCONT signal. The fg and bg utilities shall send a SIGCONT signal to the process group of the process(es) whose stopped wait status caused the shell to suspend the job. If the shell has a controlling terminal, the fg utility shall send the SIGCONT signal after it has set the foreground process group ID associated with the terminal (see above). If the fg utility is used from an interactive shell to bring into the foreground a suspended job that was created from a foreground job, before it sends the SIGCONT signal the fg utility shall restore the terminal settings to the ones that the shell saved when the job was suspended.

## Context

**Resuming suspended jobs with `fg` and `bg`:** Execution of a suspended job can be continued:
- **As foreground:** Using the `fg` builtin (see `utilities/fg.html`).
- **As background:** Using the `bg` builtin (see `utilities/bg.html`) or by sending SIGCONT to the stopped processes directly.

Both `fg` and `bg` shall send a SIGCONT signal to the process group of the process(es) whose stopped status caused the suspension (`kill(-pgid, SIGCONT)`).

**`fg` specifics:**
1. If the shell has a controlling terminal, `fg` shall set the foreground process group ID first (via `tcsetpgrp`), THEN send SIGCONT.
2. If `fg` brings to foreground a suspended job that was originally a foreground job, and the shell is interactive, `fg` shall restore the terminal settings (via `tcsetattr`) that were saved when the job was suspended (see SHALL-19-11-027), BEFORE sending SIGCONT.

The ordering is critical: set terminal ownership → restore terminal settings → send SIGCONT. This prevents race conditions where the resumed job tries to access the terminal before it's properly configured.
