## Extracted Text

> In the context of the shell, a job that the shell is not waiting for before it executes further commands or, if interactive, prompts for further commands. A background job can be a job-control background job or a non-job-control background job. A job-control background job is a job that started execution (either in the background or the foreground) while job control was enabled and is currently in the background. A non-job-control background job is an asynchronous AND-OR list that started execution while job control was disabled and was assigned a job number. An implementation need not support non-job-control background jobs; that is, the shell may, but need not, assign job numbers to asynchronous AND-OR lists that start execution while job control is disabled.

## Context

**Definition — direct shell relevance.** Defines "background job" in the shell context with two distinct subtypes:

1. **Job-control background job:** A job that started execution (foreground or background) while job control was enabled and is currently in the background. This includes jobs moved to the background via `bg` or started with `&` while `set -m` is active.

2. **Non-job-control background job:** An asynchronous AND-OR list (`cmd &`) that started while job control was disabled. An implementation NEED NOT support these; the shell MAY (but need not) assign job numbers to asynchronous lists started while job control is disabled.

**Shell impact:** `meiksh` must implement job-control background jobs when job control is enabled (`set -m`, interactive shells). For non-job-control background jobs (job control disabled, e.g., scripts), `meiksh` may choose whether to assign job numbers. This affects `jobs`, `fg`, `bg`, `wait`, and `%` job specifications.

**Design decision:** Whether `meiksh` assigns job numbers to async lists started while job control is disabled. Many shells (bash, dash) do not assign job numbers in non-interactive mode.
